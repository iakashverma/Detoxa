Awareness

Has social media actually cost you something?

Respondents were asked whether social media has jeopardized their studies, finances, relationships, or career.

Reading this chart

140 of 277 respondents (50.5%) say social media has definitely jeopardized an important part of their life. Add the 67 who said "maybe," and nearly three in four respondents have at least some doubt about social media's role in their setbacks.

Only 69 respondents (24.9%) were confident it hasn't affected them at all.

Action

Trying to quit is common. Sticking with it isn't.

Among the 269 respondents who answered how often they've tried to reduce their addiction, we grouped responses into three bands: never tried, tried inconsistently (rarely/sometimes), and tried consistently (often/always).

This is the central finding of the study: the largest single group (34.6%) tries only "sometimes" — occasional, low-commitment attempts rather than a sustained habit change. Consistent quitters ("often" + "always") make up less than a third of all respondents.

Does knowing lead to doing?

Awareness vs. quit attempts

Cross-referencing "has social media jeopardized your life" against "have you tried to quit" shows that awareness raises the odds of trying — but doesn't guarantee it.

Of respondents who said "yes" social media has jeopardized their life, 56.4% (93 of 165) had still tried to quit — leaving 43.6% who recognize the harm but have not yet acted on it. Among those who said "no" harm at all, 44.3% still tried to quit anyway, often citing time management or general wellness rather than visible damage.

Usage patterns

Where the time actually goes

Primary platform

Daily time spent on social media

Instagram dominates as the primary platform (48.7%), more than double YouTube or WhatsApp. Most respondents self-report moderate use (1–3 hrs/day), though this is self-estimated, not device-tracked — actual screen time is typically higher than self-reports in this kind of survey.

What people actually did

Real quit strategies, cleaned from free-text answers

Respondents who'd tried to quit were asked, in their own words, what they did. Of 205 free-text responses, 50 were unusable (jokes, single letters, "n/a"). The remaining 155 were grouped into categories below.

Deleting or uninstalling apps outright was the single most common concrete strategy (28 mentions) — more popular than soft measures like screen-time limits (9) or scheduled "digital detox" periods (5). This suggests a pattern worth flagging in your paper: when people do act, they tend to reach for blunt, all-or-nothing measures rather than moderation tools — which may partly explain why so few sustain the change (binary measures are harder to maintain than gradual ones).

A note on this chart

Anxiety (47.5% of the 59 respondents) and depression (30.5%) were the most-cited symptoms attributed to social media use. We're flagging the small base size clearly here — this is suggestive, not conclusive, and should be framed that way in the paper.
