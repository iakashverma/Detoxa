Methodology & scope

About this study

Why we moved past "social media harms mental health" — and what we measured instead.

The starting point

This research began with a broader question — the effect of social media on mental health. That question has been studied extensively, and the directional answer is well established: heavy, unmanaged use is associated with anxiety, comparison, distraction, and sleep disruption. Re-confirming that finding alone wouldn't add much.

So the focus shifted to a narrower, less-studied question: given that most people already sense social media is affecting them, how many actually try to do something about it — and how many succeed? That's the question this dataset and site are built to answer.

Sample & collection

  • Total responses collected277
  • Respondent typeStudents (primary), faculty, support staff, working professionals
  • Collection methodReal-time structured survey, self-reported
  • Survey length25 questions covering demographics, usage patterns, and dependency indicators
  • Gender split185 male · 92 female
  • Occupation split229 students · 16 private sector · 14 other · 8 business · 8 teachers · 2 government

What we measured

The survey instrument captured three layers, in this order:

1. Usage patterns — platform preference, daily hours, time loss while scrolling, purposeless use.

2. Psychological indicators — distractibility, concentration difficulty, social comparison, sleep disruption, mood, restlessness without access, appearance-related comparison, and concealment of usage (a known proxy for problematic use in behavioral-addiction research).

3. Awareness and action — whether respondents believe social media has jeopardized their studies, finances, relationships, or career; whether they've tried to quit or reduce use; how often; and, in their own words, what they actually did.

Data cleaning

Survey data — especially from open text fields and an anonymous, low-stakes form — always contains noise. We applied two corrections, both disclosed rather than silently absorbed:

Age: 56 of 277 responses had implausible ages (clustering at a value inconsistent with a student/professional sample) and were excluded from age-specific statistics only — they remain in every other metric.

Free-text "measures taken": of 205 responses to "what did you do to quit," 50 were unusable (single letters, jokes, "n/a") and were excluded. The remaining 155 were grouped into six categories by keyword matching, reviewed for sense.

Limitations

Self-report bias. Usage hours, symptoms, and quit attempts are all self-estimated, not device-tracked. People tend to underestimate screen time and may over- or under-report quit attempts depending on social desirability.

Sample skew. The sample is heavily student-weighted (83%) and India-based, so findings should be read as representative of that population, not a general claim about all social media users.

Sparse symptom data. Only 59 of 277 respondents answered the symptom question. That breakdown (anxiety, depression, ADHD, other) is presented as a share of respondents who answered, not the full sample, and should be treated as suggestive rather than conclusive.

Correlation, not causation. The relationship between awareness and action (people who feel jeopardized are somewhat more likely to try quitting) is associative. The survey design can't establish that one causes the other.

How to cite
